1. Land Selection: Choose a well-drained, loamy soil with good fertility. Wheat prefers slightly acidic to neutral soil with a pH of 6.0-7.5. Ensure the land receives adequate sunlight.

2. Variety Selection: Select a wheat variety suitable for your region and climate. Consult local agricultural experts or your nearest agricultural extension office for recommendations.

3. Soil Preparation: Clear the land of any weeds, rocks, or debris. Plow the field to a depth of 15-20 cm (6-8 inches) and then harrow it to create a fine seedbed.

4. Seedbed Preparation: Use a seedbed roller or plank the field to create a firm, level surface. Remove any remaining weeds, rocks, or clumps from the seedbed.

5. Seeding: Wheat is typically sown using a seed drill or by broadcasting. The recommended seeding rate varies by variety, but a common range is 100-150 kg of seed per hectare. Plant wheat seeds at a depth of 2-3 cm (1 inch) and ensure even spacing between rows.

6. Fertilization: Conduct a soil test to determine nutrient deficiencies and apply fertilizers accordingly. Wheat requires nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. Apply fertilizers in two or more split doses, with the first dose at sowing and subsequent doses during the crop's growth stages.

7. Irrigation: Provide adequate irrigation during the wheat's growth stages. Wheat needs moisture especially during the tillering and flowering stages. Monitor soil moisture and avoid over-irrigation, which can lead to diseases.

8. Weed Control: Keep the field free of weeds that can compete with wheat for nutrients and sunlight. Use herbicides, manual weeding, or mechanical weeders as necessary.

9. Disease and Pest Management: Monitor the crop for signs of diseases like rust, powdery mildew, and pests such as aphids and armyworms. Apply appropriate pesticides or biological control measures as needed.
